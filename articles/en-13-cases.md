## The Dust Bowl — Stripping the Great Plains of Topsoil

The 1930s. The American Midwest.
**The Dust Bowl** was one of the greatest environmental disasters in human history.

:::chain
**What caused the Dust Bowl:**
The U.S. government promoted westward expansion → Settlers flooded the Great Plains
All grassland plowed into wheat fields → Topsoil accumulated over millennia was exposed
World War I drove wheat prices up → Cultivated area expanded further
The 1930s brought severe drought → Crops died, topsoil lay bare
→ **Topsoil blown away by wind, creating massive dust storms**
→ Skies turned dark, dust reached the East Coast
→ Millions lost their land and fled west
:::

:::highlight
**Scale of the Dust Bowl:**
Area affected: ~40 million hectares (larger than Japan's total land area)
Topsoil lost: estimated billions of tons
People displaced: over 2.5 million
Duration: ~10 years (1930–1940)
Cause: Unplanned plowing of grasslands
:::

Grasslands were ecosystems built over millennia by grasses and large herbivores.
One generation stripped away the topsoil.

And America did not learn from the Dust Bowl.

:::chain
**After the Dust Bowl:**
Instead of restoring topsoil → Compensated with chemical fertilizer
Instead of regenerating soil → Pumped irrigation water
Instead of restoring ecosystems → Killed pests with chemicals
→ The Ogallala Aquifer heads toward depletion
→ Topsoil loss continues
→ **The same mistake repeated by different means**
:::

## Punjab — The "Green Revolution" Killed the Soil

The 1960s. India faced famine.
**The Green Revolution** saved India — so the story goes.

High-yield varieties, chemical fertilizer, pesticides, irrigation.
Punjab became India's breadbasket. Food production soared.

**But look at the reality 50 years later.**

:::chain
**Punjab today:**
Massive chemical fertilizer → Soil microbes dead → Soil reduced to "dead growing medium"
Massive pesticide spraying → Groundwater contaminated → Cancer rates soaring
Irrigation dependency → Water table dropping 1+ meter per year
Monoculture (wheat-rice rotation) → Soil nutrients depleted unevenly
Stubble burning → Air pollution → Smoke reaches Delhi
:::

:::highlight
**The true cost of the "Green Revolution":**
Punjab's farmer suicide rate is the highest in India.
Buried in debt, buying ever more fertilizer and pesticides,
watching yields fall as soil dies, increasing inputs further,
debt spiraling, unable to repay, taking their own lives.
**This is the reality of the "Green Revolution" after 50 years.**
:::

The Dust Bowl and Punjab share the same structure.
Great power pursued "efficiency," ignored nature's cycles, and killed the soil.

## The Soviet Tragedy — Central Planning Kills Agriculture

:::chain
**Soviet destruction of agriculture:**
Stalin ordered agricultural collectivization (1929)
→ All private farmland seized, merged into kolkhozes (collective farms)
→ Farmers' knowledge and autonomy completely eliminated
→ Bureaucrats in Moscow decided what to plant and when
→ People who had never seen a field "planned" agriculture
→ **Mass famine struck Ukraine (Holodomor)**
→ **An estimated 4–7 million people starved to death**
:::

Ukraine was called "the breadbasket of Europe" — some of the most fertile land on Earth.
World-class chernozem (black soil) stretched across the country.
**On that land, people starved.**

:::highlight
**The structure of the Holodomor:**
The land was fertile. The farmers were capable.
But central power seized the land from the farmers,
seized autonomy from the farmers, seized grain from the farmers.
Food existed — Moscow requisitioned it.
**People were not killed by food shortage. They were killed by power.**
:::

Then came the Lysenko affair.

:::chain
**The Lysenko affair — power kills science:**
Trofim Lysenko → An "agricultural scientist" who pleased Stalin
Rejected Mendelian genetics → Claimed "environment changes traits"
Won Stalin's backing → Dominated Soviet agricultural science
Geneticists who disagreed were imprisoned or executed → Scientific criticism silenced
Lysenko's methods forced on the entire nation → **Agricultural productivity collapsed**

:::

Lysenko was a "researcher."
But not a practitioner in the field — a desk worker who courted power.
**Researchers close to power are more dangerous than practitioners.**
